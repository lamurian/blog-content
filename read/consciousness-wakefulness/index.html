Introduction

…every one who is awake perceives either some external movement or else some movement in his own consciousness…a soulless body has not the potentiality of perception.

— Aristotle, on Sleep and Sleeplessness

A question on consciousness, and consequently free will, is an enigmatic and inexplicable query. Are we all beings controlled by homunculus and/or the imp of perversion? Do we comply with the view of our thought, vis-à-vis, what Descartes summarized as cogito ergo sum? Numerous studies and thought experiments attempted to delineate such queries resulted in the propagation of dismal rationalization, whereby said questions regarded as hard-to-solve problems. This review, highly influenced by Cleeremans et al. (2019), aims to discuss proposed models in explaining consciousness published in the past five years.

First-order Representation

In first-order theory, consciousness divided into access conscious and phenomenal conscious, where Mehta and Mashour (2013) further elaborated their arguments on how phenomenal consciousness could interpret current experimental findings by the time of their publication. Phenomenal consciousness refers to an event of heightened awareness of certain environmental changes. Such changes may occur alongside general and specific representation as percepts, where general percepts explain what state ones currently in and how they differ from being unconscious. Meanwhile, specific percepts represent differences in one stimulus from others.

Said interpretation in specific percepts resemble dictionaries with key-value pairs, where the associated key would grant access to a defined value. Akin to the actual dictionary, one can associate a simple terminology (e.g. “book”) with a definition (e.g. “a set of written sheets of skin or paper or tablets of wood or ivory”). However, instead of utilizing a hard-coded key, Mehta and Mashour (2013) suggested a context-derived key from its paired value. It implies a level of abstraction during key registration in our conscious mind, i.e. when we perceive a book we would not simply imagine a set of written sheets of paper; rather we would represent the book as a concept of such a definition. Hypothetically, neural correlates on the first-order consciousness arise from sensory systems mediated by thalamocortical relay, suggesting the presence of reciprocal loops during sensory processing, resulting in information integration (León-Domı́nguez and León-Carrión, 2019).

Higher-order Representation

Higher-order representation happened in a post-sensory system (León-Domı́nguez and León-Carrión, 2019), which enable first-order representation monitoring. Such monitoring results in inner awareness (Brown, Lau and LeDoux, 2019), akin to a meta-representation on lower-order ones. The necessity of having higher-order representation lies in the inability of first-order representation to completely engender consciousness. As such, higher-order representation is an umbrella term defining awareness importance in generating a conscious experience. The process underlying higher-order representation itself does not equate with consciousness for it happens unconsciously. Consciousness emerges as an outcome from such a process, denoted by a certainty in perceiving preceding events. Inherently, metacognition poses a direct relationship with said representation, where higher-order representation potentially elicits metacognition.

Global Workspace

Global workspace, as a part of working memory, emerges information into globally available frameworks for further mental processing (Baars, 2005; Shea and Frith, 2019). Consciousness, alongside attention, would selectively allow represented information to enter the global workspace in a goal-directed fashion. Interestingly, said working memory does not require sustained neural activity in the prefrontal cortex (PFC), implying dynamic information representation (Stokes, 2015). Dynamic working memory formation shall assume short-term synaptic plasticity (STSP), where encoded information can undergo abrupt disconnection without affecting the memory registry (Stokes, 2015). Considering consciousness as a necessary apparatus affecting the mental process, such findings may suggest consciousness as having other roles rather than merely maintaining working memory within the global workspace.

Reflection on a particular mental state is an important facet in having conscious experiences (Shea and Frith, 2019), where such a reflection may arise from metacognition, i.e. the ability to recognize cognitive process outputs (Fleming and Lau, 2014). Therefore, Shea and Frith (2019) hypothesized a certain degree of metacognition parameters involvement in cognitive process-related mental tasks served by the global workspace to ensure effective performance. In a task-driven activity, a subject may require information to undergo three processes of input, transition and output. During input, information emerges as a conscious event through a global broadcast into the dedicated workspace. The transition phase is the manipulation and comparison of such information, whereby metacognition parameters becomes indispensable, especially confidence judgment in determining certainty and uncertainty (Kepecs and Mainen, 2012; Shea and Frith, 2019). Finally, the output process will express processed information as an action or verbal report. Conscious access to the global workspaces marked by elevated activity in the frontoparietal and medial temporal cortex (Baars, 2005). The presence of metacognition is necessary to invoke inner awareness on the ongoing mental process, where such a presence may generate higher-order representation from the general workspace.

Quantum Theory and Consciousness

Based on observer’s effect postulate in quantum mechanics, quantum realms received attention in its potential to elaborate consciousness and merge the body-mind duality. Quantum tunnelling effects on neurotransmitter exocytosis (Georgiev and Glazebrook, 2018), psychons (Eccles, 1992) as mental microunits transmitting consciousness-related information (Beck and Eccles, 1992) and Orchestrated Objective-Reduction (Orch-OR) (Hameroff and Penrose, 2014) are but a few of numerous proposals in illuminating the hard question. Chemical connection on the synaptic bouton happens due to neurotransmitter exocytosis, which releases rate influenced by momentary probability increase following selections of events (quantum tunnelling) process. Psychons, as microunits ubiquitously present in the nervous system, would selectively propagate encoded consciousness-related information from pyramidal cells to apical dendrites. Meanwhile, Orch-OR theory proposed microtubules vibration, resulting in quantum interference superposition and collapse. Initially, quantum vibration starts due to ionic composition changes-related phosphorylation at a very short distance approaching zero of \(d: d \to 0\), where \(d\) eventually widen during an infinitesimal amount of time, resulting in superposition. Subsequently, superposed mass-energy will accumulate and reaching a certain threshold of quantum gravity. Considering microtubule as a nanocomputer with self-stabilizing logic algorithms, such a superposition would stabilize and collapse into one definitive state, where Hameroff and Penrose (2014) proposed said state as a discrete and independent conscious moment (Li et al., 2019).

Conclusion

Albeit myriad theories on consciousness, we are yet to achieve concrete neurobiological correlate on this particular day-to-day experience. Contributions from first-order and higher-order representation alongside global workspace as well as quantum theory have widened our perspective in understanding the nature of consciousness. However, could the hard question on consciousness be chimeric? To conclude this review, I would present a quote from Dennett (2018):

The key to a unified account (of consciousness and free will) lies…in the recognition…that open-endedness of human brain’s representational power is…both the problem and the solution…

— Daniel C. Dennett, Facing up to the hard question of consciousness
